## Section 5 - Balance Sheet Analysis

### A. Efficiency & Cash Conversion Cycle

| Metric | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 prov | | Popees (FY26) | Lux Industries | FirstCry |
|--------|------|------|-----------|--|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Debtor Days | 140 | 137 | 97 | | 97 | ~95-110 | ~15-25 |
| Inventory Days | 160 | 196 | 268 | | 268 | ~150-180 | ~60-80 |
| Creditor Days | 105 | 119 | 75 | | 75 | ~30-45 | ~45-60 |
| CCC (days) | 194 | 214 | 290 | | 290 | ~250-290 | ~30-60 |

(Days computed on a 365-day basis; FY26 is a full 12-month provisional period so no part-period annualisation is needed. Peer figures indicative, to be confirmed in diligence.)

The cash conversion cycle is the defining feature of this balance sheet and it is deteriorating: ~194 -> 214 -> 290 days. Debtor days actually improved in FY26 (collections better, 97 vs 137), but inventory days blew out to 268 (inventory Rs 79.25 Cr, of which finished goods Rs 63.55 Cr) and creditor days shrank to 75, so the company is now funding ~290 days of operating cycle. That is a red CCC band (>120 days) - every rupee of growth ties up cash. The level is partly sector-structural - knitwear/apparel manufacturers like Lux run CCCs in the 250-290 day range because of seasonal finished-goods stocking - so Popees is not an outlier among manufacturers, but it is a world away from asset-light baby retailers like FirstCry. The point for a lender: this business consumes working capital to grow, which is exactly why it carries bank limits and why its free cash flow is thin.

### B. Working Capital Quality

| Check | Finding | Read |
|-------|---------|------|
| Profit real? | Debtor days improving (140 -> 97), but inventory days stretching hard (160 -> 268); a Rs 3.83 Cr bad-debt write-off in FY25 | Concern - profit is accrual-real but increasingly parked in inventory and advances |
| dWC / Revenue growth | FY26 revenue grew Rs 26.7 Cr; working-capital cash movement (per CFS) was a release of ~Rs 18 Cr as supplier advances unwound, so dWC/growth is favourable this year, but FY25 was the reverse (Rs ~40 Cr WC build on Rs 13 Cr growth) | Mixed - volatile year to year, driven by the large swing in supplier advances |
| ROCE > cost of funding? | FY26 ROCE 22.3% vs CoD ~16.8% | OK - capital earns above its cost |

Overall working-capital quality is the main caution. The headline ratios are distorted by two unusual current-asset lines: advance to suppliers of Rs 22.87 Cr (FY26; was Rs 39.88 Cr in FY25) and a loan to the director of Rs 6.58 Cr (up from Rs 2.07 Cr). These are not normal trade working capital. The FY25 advance-to-suppliers spike consumed the equity raise; its FY26 unwind is what made OCF positive again. Until the nature, ageing and recoverability of those advances and the director loan are understood, the quality of the balance sheet is unproven.

### C. Return on Capital vs Cost of Debt

| Year | ROCE | CoD | ROCE - CoD | Earnings cover interest? | Stage |
|------|------|-----|------------|--------------------------|-------|
| FY25 | 15.5% | 16.6% | -1.1% | Yes (PBT Rs 10.4 Cr; EBIT interest cover 2.7x) | 3 |
| FY26 prov | 22.3% | 16.8% | +5.5% | Yes (PBT Rs 23.6 Cr; EBIT interest cover 5.8x) | 1 |

Incremental: NOPLAT growth Rs 10.33 Cr / capital added Rs 19.85 Cr = incremental ROCE 52% vs CoD ~17% -> well above cost. On an accounting basis the business creates value with new capital, and FY26 sits firmly in Stage 1 (ROCE above cost of debt, earnings cover interest comfortably). The caveat carried from Section 3 is that this is accounting return; on a cash basis interest cover is below 1x because returns are locked in working capital. Cost of debt of ~17% is high (cash-credit at 12.75% plus higher-rate state-FI and term lines) - a lower-cost facility that genuinely displaces the dearer lines would itself be accretive.

### D. Liquidity

| Ratio | Value | Benchmark | Flag |
|-------|-------|-----------|------|
| Current Ratio | 2.70x | > 1.2x | OK on the face of it |
| Quick Ratio | 1.32x | > 1.0x | OK on the face of it |

Both ratios clear their benchmarks, but they overstate true liquidity. Strip out inventory and the quick ratio still leans on Rs 42.91 Cr of receivables and Rs 22.87 Cr of supplier advances; actual cash is only Rs 0.61 Cr against Rs 20.67 Cr of short-term borrowings. The company is liquid on paper and tight in cash - it survives on the rollover of its working-capital limits, not on a cash buffer.

### E. Source & Use of Funds (FY26 provisional)

| | Short-term (Rs Cr) | Long-term (Rs Cr) |
|---|---|---|
| Sources | ST borrowings 20.67 + trade payables 22.22 + other CL 9.41 + ST provisions 5.32 = 57.62 | Equity & reserves 104.15 + LT borrowings 3.27 + other LT liab (distributor deposits) 11.19 = 118.61 |
| Uses | Receivables 42.91 + inventory 79.25 + cash 0.61 + other CA (supplier advances) 22.88 + ST loans (incl. director loan) 9.66 = 155.31 | Fixed assets 12.33 + intangibles 4.57 + DTA 0.60 + other NCA 3.40 = 20.90 |

ST sources Rs 57.62 Cr vs ST uses Rs 155.31 Cr - short-term uses far exceed short-term sources, with the Rs 97.7 Cr gap funded by long-term money (mostly the equity base). That is the conservative direction (long-term funding short-term assets), so there is no maturity mismatch of the dangerous kind. The structural issue is not the funding direction but the sheer size of the working-capital asset block relative to the business.

Concentration is the headline revenue-quality flag: the single largest account is 39% of receivables, and the top five are ~70%. The broader retail/marketplace tail is healthy and diversified, but the top of the book is concentrated in a handful of distributors, two of which (Veliyath, Ennexa) and the UAE account (Fazza) carry meaningful >180-day balances. No customer-wise revenue sheet was provided, so concentration is proxied from the debtor ageing and must be reconfirmed on a revenue basis in diligence.
