## Section 0 - Company, Industry and Founder Overview

### About the Company

Popees is a Kerala-based babywear and baby-care brand that manufactures and sells infant and toddler garments, plus a growing range of baby FMCG and accessories, to Indian (and some GCC) consumers. It is vertically integrated: own and leased garment factories in Kerala, Karnataka and Tirupur feed a multi-channel network of ~105 exclusive and franchise stores alongside distributor wholesale and online marketplaces. Revenue is overwhelmingly product sales (transactional), part B2C through owned and franchise stores and part B2B through distributors and large-format retail.

| Vertical | What it is | % of revenue |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Baby & kids apparel | Core knitted/woven babywear, in-house manufactured; sold via EBOs, franchise, wholesale, online | Dominant (majority); not separately disclosed |
| Baby FMCG | Diapers, lotion, bamboo wipes, soap, fabric wash; mostly third-party manufactured under the Popees brand | Growing minority; n.a. |
| Accessories / supportives | Teethers, bottles, strollers, soft toys, hospital and maternity kits | Small; n.a. |

Business model: product sales, transactional, across COCO, FICO (12% commission on net sales), FOCO and FOFO store formats, plus distributor wholesale and e-commerce (Ajio, Amazon, Meesho). Stage: mature and scaling, about Rs 162 Cr revenue (FY26 provisional), profitable, crossed Rs 100 Cr turnover around 2021.

(Legal and ownership: standalone public limited company, recently converted from private limited; promoter Shaju Thomas holds 73.76%. Part of a 12-entity Popees Group, several of which are being merged into PBCPL across FY26-FY27. Standalone financials used; consolidated not provided. From company_structure.md.)

### About the Industry

India's baby and mother-care market - babywear plus baby FMCG - is large and structurally growing, supported by roughly 143 million children aged 0-6, rising incomes, urbanisation and a shift from unbranded to branded babywear. Organised penetration is still low, which is the growth thesis, but it is a crowded value segment. (Market-size figures here are indicative, drawn from the deck and general knowledge, to be confirmed against a sector report in diligence.)

Vintage: the Popees brand has run for about 20 years (founded 2003, first factory 2005 per the deck), though the legal entity PBCPL dates to 2016 (~10 years). That is a long vintage for a regional babywear brand and implies real brand equity in South India, established manufacturing know-how, and survival through several demand cycles including COVID. Resilience reads as moderate-to-strong regionally, less proven outside South India where the store roll-out is only now beginning.

| Player archetype | Example(s) | Business model in one line |
|------------------|-----------|----------------------------|
| Organised baby-retail platforms | FirstCry (listed) | Omni-channel mother-and-baby marketplace plus private labels, national scale |
| Listed knitwear / apparel majors | Lux Industries, Rupa, Dollar, Page Industries | Branded knitted garments, manufacturing-led, gross margins ~30-55% |
| Branded kidswear specialists | Gini & Jony, Hopscotch, Nino Bambino | Kidswear brands, mix of retail and online, mostly unlisted |
| Regional / unorganised | local babywear makers, FOCO/FOFO operators | Price-led, fragmented, limited brand - the segment Popees is consolidating |

The competitive reality is that this is a value-segment, trend-sensitive category where a few well-capitalised national players (FirstCry above all) and a long tail of unorganised makers both press on price. The defensible edge has to come from in-house manufacturing cost control, brand trust and store economics rather than from pricing power.

## Section 3 - Cash Flow Analysis

For a debt fund, repayment comes from cash. The question is whether the business throws off enough real cash to service (ICR) and retire (Debt to FCF) its borrowings without leaning on refinancing. OCF below is stated before interest, on the actual cash flow statements.

| Metric | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 (prov) | Threshold | Flag |
|--------|------|------|-------------|-----------|------|
| OCF (Rs Cr) | 6.49 | (39.74) | 6.56 | n.a. | FY25 deeply negative |
| Capex (Rs Cr) | (0.47) | (0.09) | (3.22) | n.a. | |
| FCF (Rs Cr) | 6.01 | (39.83) | 3.35 | over 0 | FY25 negative |
| Total Debt (Rs Cr) | 38.09 | 29.54 | 23.94 | n.a. | Falling - good |
| Debt / FCF | 6.34x | n.m. | 7.15x | 6x or below | Flag - above 6x; FY25 not meaningful (FCF negative) |
| ICR, cash basis (FCF / Interest) | 0.85x | (6.31x) | 0.68x | 1x or above | Flag - below 1x every year |

Our read: this is the central weakness. Popees is profitable on the P&L and is steadily de-levering (debt Rs 38.1 Cr to Rs 29.5 Cr to Rs 23.9 Cr), but it does not generate enough free cash to cover its own interest in cash terms - cash ICR is below 1x in all three years, and Debt/FCF sits above the 6x comfort line whenever FCF is positive. The cause is working capital. FY25 operations consumed Rs 39.7 Cr of cash (an inventory build plus a Rs 36 Cr jump in advances to suppliers), funded almost entirely by a ~Rs 54 Cr equity raise. FY26 swings back to +Rs 6.6 Cr OCF as some advances unwind, but after Rs 3.2 Cr of store capex the free cash left (Rs 3.35 Cr) still does not fully cover Rs 4.96 Cr of interest. So debt today is serviced out of fresh equity, the unwinding of working capital, and rolling bank limits rather than out of durable free cash flow. For our facility this is the thing to structure around: cash conversion has to improve for the business to self-fund debt service. (DSCR is dropped; with long-term principal assumed refinanced it collapses to ICR.)

## Section 5 - Balance Sheet Analysis

How the business is funded and how efficiently capital is used, and whether short-term money is being stretched to cover long-term assets.

### A. Efficiency and Cash Conversion Cycle

| Metric | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 prov | | Popees (FY26) | Lux Industries | FirstCry |
|--------|------|------|-----------|--|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Debtor Days | 140 | 137 | 97 | | 97 | ~95-110 | ~15-25 |
| Inventory Days | 160 | 196 | 268 | | 268 | ~150-180 | ~60-80 |
| Creditor Days | 105 | 119 | 75 | | 75 | ~30-45 | ~45-60 |
| CCC (days) | 194 | 214 | 290 | | 290 | ~250-290 | ~30-60 |

The cash conversion cycle is the defining feature of this balance sheet and it is deteriorating: ~194, 214, 290 days. Debtor days actually improved in FY26 (97 vs 137), but inventory days blew out to 268 (inventory Rs 79.25 Cr, finished goods Rs 63.55 Cr) and creditor days shrank to 75, so the company now funds ~290 days of operating cycle. That is a red CCC band (over 120 days), and rising. The level is partly sector-structural - manufacturers like Lux run 250-290 day CCCs because of seasonal finished-goods stocking - so Popees is not an outlier among makers, but it is a world away from asset-light baby retail. The lender point: this business consumes working capital to grow, which is why it carries bank limits and why its free cash flow is thin (peer figures indicative).

### B. Working-Capital Quality

| Check | Finding | Read |
|-------|---------|------|
| Profit real? | Debtor days improving (140 to 97), but inventory days stretching (160 to 268); Rs 3.83 Cr bad-debt write-off in FY25 | Concern - accrual-real but parked in inventory and advances |
| dWC over Revenue growth | FY26 saw a ~Rs 18 Cr WC release as supplier advances unwound; FY25 was the reverse (~Rs 40 Cr build on Rs 13 Cr growth) | Concern - volatile, driven by the supplier-advance swing |
| ROCE over cost of funding? | FY26 ROCE 22.3% vs CoD ~16.8% | OK - capital earns above its cost |

Working-capital quality is the main caution. The ratios are distorted by two non-trade current-asset lines: advance to suppliers Rs 22.87 Cr (FY26; Rs 39.88 Cr in FY25) and a loan to the director Rs 6.58 Cr (up from Rs 2.07 Cr). The FY25 advance spike consumed the equity raise; its FY26 unwind is what turned OCF positive. Until the nature, ageing and recoverability of those advances and the director loan are understood, the balance-sheet quality is unproven.

## Recommendation

We shall proceed with due diligence, with conditions.

Popees is a genuinely investable business: a ~20-year babywear brand with real equity in South India, vertically integrated manufacturing, positive and improving unit economics (FY26 CM2 28.9%), expanding margins (EBITDA 9.4% to 18.5% over three years), low leverage (D/TNW 0.49x, D/E 0.23x) and a clean, bank-only lender profile. It clears Section 1 outright and passes Section 2 at four of six. What holds us back from an unqualified yes is cash, not profit. The business converts profit into cash poorly: a ~290-day cash conversion cycle, only Rs 0.61 Cr of balance-sheet cash, free cash flow that does not cover interest, and a heavy dependence on rolling bank limits. Layered on that are two governance and quality items we must clear before funding - a 39% single-customer concentration in receivables (BR Trading) and a Rs 6.58 Cr loan to a director - plus the fact that the strong FY26 figures are provisional and not yet internally consistent. A Rs 30 Cr venture-debt cheque into a business with this little cash and this much working capital has to be tightly structured, with most of it ring-fenced for the working-capital and store-expansion uses it is raised for.

Strengths
- Long-vintage, trusted regional babywear brand with vertically integrated, cost-controlled manufacturing (pitch deck; ~105 stores; ~20-year history).
- Positive, healthy unit economics - FY26 CM2 of 28.9% and gross margin ~33% (MIS, FY26 provisional).
- Clear operating leverage - EBITDA margin up from 9.4% (FY24) to 18.5% (FY26 provisional) as fixed costs fall with scale.
- Low leverage and a clean lender base - D/TNW 0.49x (FY25 audited), D/E 0.23x (FY26), all debt from two scheduled banks and NSIC, no NBFC/AIF/promoter debt.
- Strong accounting returns - FY26 ROCE 22% and incremental ROCE ~52%, both above the ~17% cost of debt.

Weaknesses
- Free cash flow does not cover interest in cash terms (cash ICR below 1x every year); debt is currently serviced from equity, working-capital unwind and limit rollover, not durable cash.
- Razor-thin liquidity (Rs 0.61 Cr cash against Rs 20.67 Cr short-term borrowings; base-case runway ~4.6 months).
- Rising working-capital intensity - CCC ~290 days, inventory days 268; high even allowing for the knitwear sector, where peers like Lux run 250-290 days.
- Receivables concentration - BR Trading Manjeri at 39% of the book, top five ~70%, with aged balances at two distributors and a UAE account.
- Related-party and quality items on the asset side - Rs 6.58 Cr director loan and Rs 22.87 Cr supplier advances of unproven nature and recoverability.

Areas for further deep diligence
- Audited FY26 numbers reconciled to the provisional MIS, the deck, and GST/sales records - the profitability step-up must be verified before it is relied on.
- BR Trading Manjeri - ownership, relationship to the promoters, terms, and collection track record; customer concentration re-tested on a revenue (not just receivables) basis.
- The Rs 6.58 Cr director loan and the Rs 22.87 Cr supplier advances - purpose, counterparties, ageing, recoverability, and a plan/covenant to unwind.
- Inventory ageing (not disclosed) and the Rs 63.55 Cr finished-goods position - obsolescence and realisability, given 268 inventory days.
- Working-capital trajectory and a true monthly cash-flow / runway picture; sanctioned vs utilised bank limits and renewal status across Federal, Union and NSIC.
- Group structure - the 12 related entities, the planned mergers, intra-group pricing, and whether consolidated financials change the read.

Suggested structure or mitigants
- Ring-fence use of proceeds to the stated working-capital and store-expansion uses, with milestone-based or tranched disbursement tied to store roll-out.
- A debt-service reserve (one to two quarters of interest) given cash ICR below 1x and Rs 0.61 Cr of cash, plus an escrow/sweep on collections from the top distributors.
- Covenants: a cap on related-party outflows (no further increase in the director loan or in advances to suppliers without consent), a single-customer receivable concentration cap, a minimum DSCR/interest-cover test, and a maximum CCC or inventory-days covenant.
- Security: a charge ranking alongside the existing book-debt/stock hypothecation as available, personal guarantees from the promoters (consistent with their property already pledged to the banks), and a negative lien on the unencumbered fixed assets.
- Confirm tenure and security offered (both not provided in the deck) before we move to terms.
